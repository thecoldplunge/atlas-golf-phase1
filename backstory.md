# Backstory

## Earth and the Interstellar Tour

Earth is proud but conflicted about its Tour players. Funding is limited, public support is passionate but fickle, and there's an ongoing cultural debate about whether humanity's best and brightest should be chasing a ball across alien worlds or solving problems at home.

## Lore Hooks

1. The last human to reach the final round of The Keldaran Masters, Joaquin Reyes, retired mid-tournament in 2184 under mysterious circumstances. His Tour file is sealed. His caddie hasn't spoken publicly since.

2. A Paxi player named Thresh recently accused the Voss Hegemony of engineering their gravity-adapted clubs to exploit a loophole in Neutral Compact equipment regs. The Tour council is investigating. Tensions are rising.

3. There's a decommissioned course on Nyx-4, a dead moon in the Keldaran system, that was removed from Tour certification after three players disappeared during a practice round. Locals say the terrain shifts.

4. FoldRight, the largest fold-transit corporation, is offering a record-breaking sponsorship deal to the first human to win a Major this season. The catch: exclusive travel rights, meaning they control where and when you compete.

5. The Rill Consortium has never lost The Rill Invitational on home ice. There's a saying on Tour: "Nobody beats a Rill in the cold." Seven species have tried. The streak is forty-one years running.

6. A black-market fabricator on Aeris Station claims to have a prototype putter made from compressed neutron-star alloy. It's almost certainly illegal under Compact regs. It's also supposedly the most precise instrument ever built.

7. Earth's Sol Classic is considered the weakest of the four Majors by alien pundits, who find the single-gravity, oxygen-atmosphere conditions "quaint." Human fans take this personally. The atmosphere at the Sol Classic is the most hostile in professional golf, not because of the course, but because of the crowd.

8. A retired Voss champion, Dorran Kael, now runs a training compound on a high-gravity station where he's allegedly coaching a human prospect in Voss power techniques. The Hegemony considers this a cultural betrayal.

9. Fold-lag, the neurological side effect of repeated space compression, is an open secret on Tour. Long-haul players develop micro-tremors, depth perception drift, and what caddies call "the yips between stars." There is no official treatment. The Tour does not acknowledge it exists.

10. Every fifty years, the Neutral Compact allows a provisional course from a non-signatory world to host an exhibition major. The next window opens this season. Three uncontacted civilizations have submitted bids. Nobody knows what their courses look like. Nobody knows what they look like.
